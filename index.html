That's exciting! However, getting this specific app to the App Store has a few big hurdles worth knowing about upfront.

⚠️ The main issue — Apple's gambling rules

Apple is very strict about apps involving real money + chance-based outcomes (like pack openings). Your app would fall under gambling/loot box regulations, which means:

  • Apple requires a gambling license in every region you operate
  • Apps with real-money random rewards are frequently rejected or pulled
  • You'd need to comply with laws in each country (some ban loot boxes entirely, like Belgium and the Netherlands)

✅ What you CAN do to get it approved:

The most common workaround is making it a free-to-play virtual currency only app — no real money in, no real money out. Many card games (like Pokémon TCG Live itself) do this successfully.

🛠️ To actually build a real iOS app, you'd need:

  1. A Mac + Xcode (Apple's dev tool, free)
  2. Apple Developer Account — $99/year at developer.apple.com
  3. Convert this web app into a Swift/SwiftUI app or wrap it in a WKWebView (easiest path from what we built)
  4. A backend — user accounts, card databases, real payment processing (Stripe)
  5. Submit for App Store Review (takes 1–3 days typically)

🚀 Easiest real path forward:

The quickest way to ship something real would be to wrap this as a Progressive Web App (PWA) — users add it to their home screen from Safari, it feels like an app, and you avoid App Store restrictions entirely.

🌐 Host it as a website The simplest option. Put your app online at a URL like pokepacks.com and anyone can use it from their phone browser. It works on iOS and Android — no download needed.

📲 Progressive Web App (PWA) A step up from a regular website. Users visit your site, tap "Add to Home Screen" in Safari/Chrome, and it installs like an app with its own icon. No App Store involved. This is honestly the fastest way to get real users.

🤖 Google Play Store (Android) Android is much more relaxed than Apple. You can publish gambling/loot box apps on Google Play with far fewer restrictions, and it only costs a one-time $25 fee (vs Apple's $99/year).
